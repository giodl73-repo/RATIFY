# First Comparative Map

Machine record: `research/world-systems/institution-comparison.v1.json`.

The first result is that “direct democracy” is not one institution:

| System | What citizens can force | Binding? | Most useful RATIFY lesson |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Switzerland | Constitutional initiative or vote on a new law | Yes | Double majority plus legislative counterproposals |
| Uruguay | Constitutional initiative and rejection mechanisms | Yes | National popular constitutional agenda-setting at meaningful scale |
| Ireland | Deliberative recommendations and constitutional referendum | Mixed | Representative citizen learning before mass voting |
| Italy | Repeal of an existing law | Yes | Citizen veto with strong exclusions—and quorum risk |
| Taiwan | Agency consideration and public response | No | Verified digital agenda plus accountable response workflow |
| European Union | Commission consideration across member states | No | Geographic qualification without pretending signatures enact law |
| U.S. states | Statutes, amendments, repeal, or legislative referral | Usually | Real American qualification, wording, campaign, and administration evidence |

## Initial synthesis

The most promising American design is a hybrid, not a copy:

1. **Irish preparation:** a stratified citizen panel hears evidence, publishes
   reasons, alternatives, and dissent.
2. **U.S. state safeguards:** petition verification, single-subject review,
   fiscal analysis, official wording, conflict rules, audit, and certification.
3. **Swiss breadth test:** reserve a popular-plus-state double majority for
   constitutional or unusually durable decisions, then test its unequal-vote
   and territorial-veto effects explicitly.
4. **Taiwanese response duty:** proposals below enactment level can still earn a
   time-bound, public, reasoned institutional response.

## What does not transfer cleanly

- Switzerland's canton vote is not proof that Electoral College weighting is
  fair; both territorially weight citizens, and the effective inequality must
  be measured.
- Italy's turnout quorum can reward organized abstention.
- Ireland's assembly is advisory and institutionally convened; it is not a
  citizen power to make federal law.
- Taiwan and the EU guarantee attention and response, not enactment.
- State initiative authority does not imply federal initiative authority.

The comparison therefore strengthens the double-majority experiment while
making it more skeptical: the next simulation must measure not just passage,
but effective vote weight, state veto frequency, geographic polarization,
turnout strategy, and minority-rights exposure.
