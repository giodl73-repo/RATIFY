# Public-Priority Program Renewal

## Problem statement

Public programs can persist after their purpose, delivery system, or public
priority has changed. This does not by itself establish corruption. Persistence
can arise from concentrated benefits and diffuse costs, committee and agency
ownership, contracts and grants, lobbying and information asymmetry, fear of
service disruption, legal commitments, and the difficulty voters face in
comparing thousands of programs.

RATIFY should investigate corruption, self-dealing, or improper influence only
through attributable evidence. It should separately model ordinary
institutional stickiness, capture risk, and conflicts of interest.

## Return focus to what people care about

Citizens should set public priorities, desired outcomes, protection floors,
funding envelopes, and renewal conditions. They should not be expected to draft
technical statutory clauses or adjudicate individual grants and contracts.

```text
people choose ends, tradeoffs, and renewal conditions
institutions implement means under audit and rights review
```

## Renewal record

Before a program reaches a public review, an independent evidence office should
publish one comparable record:

- purpose and legal authority;
- current cost, ten-year path, and funding source;
- beneficiaries and geographic distribution;
- outcomes promised, measured, and not measured;
- alternatives, overlap, and administrative burden;
- contractors, grantees, subgrantees, and material funding flows;
- lobbying, campaign-finance, revolving-door, and conflict disclosures where
  lawfully reportable;
- audits, improper payments, confirmed fraud findings, recoveries, and open
  findings—kept as distinct categories;
- rights, continuity, contracts, and vulnerable-population constraints;
- agency, legislature, beneficiary, workforce, taxpayer, and dissenting views;
  and
- the cost and consequences of each ballot option.

## Citizen review sequence

1. A stratified citizen panel chooses the questions the evidence packet must
   answer and may subpoena or request missing public information through a
   legally authorized body.
2. Independent analysts publish a plain-language comparison and machine record.
3. Program leaders and affected communities receive a right of response.
4. Conflicts, funding sources, major campaign spending, and paid advocacy are
   visible beside the arguments they support.
5. The panel publishes majority reasoning, minority reasoning, and unresolved
   uncertainty.
6. Voters choose among renewal, reform, corrective status, consolidation,
   gradual sunset, or termination.
7. The legislature and executive implement the selected envelope, with courts
   preserving constitutional rights and lawful commitments.
8. A public audit compares the vote, implementation, cost, and outcomes.

## Anti-capture design

- No organization receives privileged standing merely because it currently
  receives public funds or contracts.
- Beneficiaries are heard directly rather than represented only by provider or
  advocacy organizations.
- Paid advocacy and material financial interests are disclosed, but disclosure
  does not substitute for evaluating the argument.
- Official evidence includes unfavorable findings and missing measures.
- Ballot qualification cannot be purchased solely through paid signature
  collection; geographic breadth and verified small-donor or citizen support
  alternatives should be tested.
- Public campaign resources should support competing views and factual review,
  not incumbent-government advocacy.

## Protection against reckless sunsets

Programs involving rights, disability support, medical continuity, pensions,
tribal commitments, active contracts, or essential system operations require
heightened review, transition plans, appeal, and continuity floors. A majority
cannot turn a budget ballot into permission to violate rights or abandon lawful
commitments.

## Initial ballot architecture

The public first allocates 100 priority points across outcomes—not agencies or
organizations. Program renewal then asks which delivery mechanisms best serve
those priorities within the voted fiscal envelope. This separates public ends
from incumbent providers and makes consolidation or replacement possible.

## Research gates

- Can ordinary voters distinguish outcomes from familiar program brands?
- Does disclosure reduce capture or merely increase ballot complexity?
- Do citizen panels resist organized-interest framing better than legislatures?
- Which renewal interval preserves accountability without creating permanent
  campaigning and administrative churn?
- Which decisions require ordinary majority, double majority, or insulation
  from direct repeal?
