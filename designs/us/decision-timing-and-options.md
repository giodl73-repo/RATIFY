# Decision Timing and Fair Option Formation

The objective is not “vote on everything.” It is to place public judgment at
the points where it adds legitimacy and direction while leaving urgent action,
technical drafting, administration, adjudication, and rights enforcement with
institutions designed for those tasks.

## Four clocks

1. **Priority clock:** Citizens periodically allocate attention and fiscal
   priority across outcomes.
2. **Renewal clock:** Staggered program portfolios receive evidence-based review
   at intervals suited to their risk and evidence cycle.
3. **Trigger clock:** Material failure, cost deviation, audit findings, or broad
   citizen support can open an early corrective review.
4. **Emergency clock:** Temporary action occurs immediately under bounded
   authority, then receives legislative review and public audit.

This avoids both permanent plebiscite and permanent autopilot.

## Who chooses the choices?

No single actor should. Agenda formation is divided among open proposal access,
objective qualification, stratified citizen synthesis, independent legal and
fiscal review, published exclusion reasons, and appeal. The status quo and
return-for-revision options remain on every renewal ballot.

The legislature may offer a counterproposal, following the useful Swiss idea,
but it may not suppress a citizen proposal that qualifies under the same public
rules.

## Recommended experimental ballot

For discrete policy packages:

```text
evidence -> open proposals -> qualification -> citizen shortlist
         -> fully financed packages -> ranked comparison
         -> winning reform vs status quo
```

For one continuous parameter:

```text
lawful range + consequences -> each voter enters value -> median + distribution
```

For a 100-point budget:

```text
each ballot sums to 100 + funding closes -> aggregate complete feasible ballots
-> publish method sensitivities and debt effects
```

RATIFY should not select the final 100-point aggregation rule until the methods
are stress-tested against polarization, strategic entries, state weighting,
minority priorities, and outliers.
