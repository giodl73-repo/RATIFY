# Pulse 03: Transfer and Consensus-Rule Review

## Role review

### Comparative Evidence Steward

The hybrid design is a bounded transfer hypothesis, not evidence that the
combined system works. Swiss double-majority practice supports studying a
popular-plus-territorial rule; it does not validate U.S. state weighting.

### Constitutional Boundary Reviewer

A binding federal citizen chamber would require constitutional analysis and
likely constitutional change. State initiative experience can inform
administration without supplying national authority. The current experiment
has no legal-effect claim.

### Democratic Legitimacy Reviewer

At fixed 52% national support, synthetic geography produced 10 to 42 state
wins. Three of six scenarios allowed the state threshold to veto the national
majority. Equal-state formal weight per House-seat proxy varies 52×; Electoral
College weight per proxy varies 2.89×. Territorial concurrence is therefore a
separate value with a measurable equality cost, not consensus by definition.

### Minority Rights Guardian

Neither a popular majority nor a double majority can authorize rights
violations. Program-renewal experiments must include protected-rights,
continuity, and vulnerable-population gates outside ordinary aggregation.

### Simulation Auditor

Pass with limits. The run is reproducible and fixes the national support target,
but House seats are only a population proxy, state rates are synthetic, and no
turnout or campaign behavior is modeled. Results establish rule sensitivity,
not likely election outcomes.

### Innovation Scout

The experiment changes the design: do not use Electoral College weighting as a
default. Keep double majority only as a comparator for unusually durable
decisions. Next test a popular majority with a geographic-distribution
diagnostic and deliberative precondition against a binding state veto.

## Decision

RATIFY's default experimental baseline is national popular equality. State
breadth is reported as a legitimacy diagnostic. A binding state threshold must
demonstrate benefits that justify its unequal formal weight and veto risk.
