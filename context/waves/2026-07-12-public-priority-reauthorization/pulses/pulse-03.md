# Pulse 03: Binary Ratification and Safeguard Review

## Design decision

The ordinary binding public vote is Yes/No: one complete, fiscally closed
challenger versus the complete current-law baseline. Numeric and multi-option
processes normally occur upstream to form the challenger.

## Role findings

### Democratic Legitimacy Reviewer

Binary ratification supplies a clear replacement mandate, but fairness depends
on agenda access and challenger selection. The status quo must not control the
qualification process, and preliminary winner selection must be published.

### Deliberation and Information Steward

Pass with a layered information design. The ballot contains the neutral
question and essential fiscal consequences; the official guide retains full
evidence, competing arguments, funding disclosures, models, and dissent.

### Minority Rights Guardian

Yes/No simplicity does not expand majority authority. Rights, lawful
commitments, essential continuity, transition, and appeal remain gates outside
ordinary ratification.

### Election Administrator

Binary final questions are materially easier to administer, explain, recount,
and certify than open numeric or large multi-option legal ballots. Litigation,
wording, and correction must finish before ballot production.

### Simulation Auditor

The next experiment should test matched Yes and No descriptions, challenger
funding salience, status-quo cost omission, and official-title framing. It must
not infer comprehension from vote completion alone.

## Decision

Adopt binary ratification as the RATIFY default. Retain voter-set numbers as an
agenda, envelope, and package-construction instrument. Require explicit legal
authorization before a numeric result is treated as self-executing law.
