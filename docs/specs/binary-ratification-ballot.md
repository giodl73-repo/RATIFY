# Binary Ratification Ballot

## Default public surface

The final binding vote is ordinarily binary:

```text
YES — adopt the reviewed policy-and-funding package
NO  — retain the current-law policy-and-funding package
```

The complexity of forming a lawful, fiscally closed challenger occurs before
the final vote. Voters should not need to solve a legislative drafting problem
in the voting booth.

## What “Yes” authorizes

A Yes vote approves one inseparable package containing:

- the policy and outcome mandate;
- benefit, eligibility, or service changes;
- implementation and transition schedule;
- taxes, contributions, fees, and named offsets;
- explicit borrowing and debt-service effects;
- rights, continuity, and appeal protections;
- forecast-correction corridor;
- expiration or next-review date; and
- public implementation and audit requirements.

If the funding does not pass, the policy does not pass. There is no separate
benefit-only vote followed by a politically easier search for money.

## What “No” means

No is not costless. The ballot must present current law as a complete baseline:

- current and projected program cost;
- current taxes, contributions, fees, and general revenue;
- current borrowing and debt-service path;
- measured outcomes and unresolved findings;
- scheduled expiration or next review; and
- consequences of leaving current law unchanged.

The ballot may not frame Yes as costly change and No as zero cost.

## How one challenger is selected

Before ratification:

1. An independent evidence record defines the baseline and lawful constraints.
2. Citizens and institutions submit complete, funded alternatives.
3. Objective qualification removes illegal, incomplete, duplicative, or
   fiscally open proposals, with reasons and appeal.
4. A stratified citizen panel deliberates and may consolidate substantially
   similar proposals.
5. Independent legal, fiscal, distributional, rights, and implementation review
   scores the finalists.
6. If several materially distinct finalists remain, a separate preference
   stage selects one challenger by a published approval, ranked, or pairwise
   rule.
7. The selected challenger faces current law in the binding Yes/No vote.

The preference stage may be held earlier or within the deliberative process.
The final legal authorization remains binary.

## Role of voter-set numbers

Numeric ballots remain useful for:

- annual priority signals;
- selecting a target tax or contribution corridor;
- setting a total spending or borrowing envelope;
- revealing intensity and acceptable tradeoffs; and
- constructing candidate packages.

They do not automatically enact a multidimensional law. The resulting number
must be converted into a reviewed, fiscally closed package and ratified Yes/No
unless the authorizing legal framework explicitly makes the numeric rule
self-executing.

## Ballot information hierarchy

### Required on the ballot

- one neutral question;
- Yes and No consequences;
- annual household-facing rates where they can be calculated responsibly;
- total annual and multiyear federal effects;
- borrowing and debt direction;
- effective date and next review; and
- a short rights and continuity statement.

### Required in the official guide

- full package and baseline ledgers;
- outcome and uncertainty evidence;
- distribution by relevant groups and regions;
- proponent, opponent, affected-community, agency, and dissenting analyses;
- material funding and conflicts behind paid advocacy;
- legal and implementation reviews; and
- machine-readable records and models.

## Question template

> Shall the United States adopt the [neutral package name], which [plain policy
> effect], financed by [plain funding summary], with an estimated [cost or
> savings], [borrowing effect], effective [date], and reviewed again in [year]?

```text
[ ] YES — adopt the package and its funding
[ ] NO  — retain current law and its projected fiscal path
```

## Fairness requirements

- Yes and No use parallel language and the same time horizon.
- The neutral title cannot use campaign slogans or incumbent program branding.
- Both sides show cost, funding, outcomes, and debt—not only the challenger.
- The official scorer and wording authority disclose methods and dissent.
- Litigation and correction deadlines occur before ballots are finalized.
- Abstention is not counted as No; avoid turnout quorums that reward boycott.
- A majority cannot use ratification to violate protected rights.
