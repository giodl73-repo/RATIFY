# Public Decision and Ballot Contract

## Design principle

Use direct numeric input when the public choice is genuinely continuous and
bounded. Use a short multi-option ballot when implementation choices interact.
Do not force a false binary and do not ask voters to draft technical law.

Every package must also satisfy `fiscally-closed-ballot.md`. Policy benefits,
funding, offsets, borrowing, debt service, and correction rules are one ballot
object—not separate votes that can produce a free lunch.

## Decision types

| Decision | Public input | Aggregation candidate | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Outcome priorities | 100 points across a stable outcome taxonomy | Compare mean, trimmed mean, and simplex median | Annual or biennial |
| Tax or contribution parameter | A number inside a disclosed lawful range | Median voter value, with distribution shown | Scheduled fiscal cycle |
| Program funding envelope | A number or bounded percentage | Median voter value | At reauthorization |
| Program delivery model | Rank 3–5 coherent packages | Ranked shortlist, then winner versus status quo | At reauthorization |
| Constitutional rule | Yes/no on reviewed final text | Popular majority; any territorial gate reported separately | Rare and scheduled |
| Emergency response | No advance mass vote | Temporary institutional action plus audit and possible retrospective review | Event-triggered |

The aggregation method is a research decision, not yet fixed. Numeric outputs
must publish the distribution, not only one national point estimate.

## When voting occurs

### Scheduled review

- Stagger programs so only a manageable portfolio reaches voters in any cycle.
- Default review intervals should reflect program type, evidence latency,
  contracts, and continuity risk rather than one universal sunset period.
- Constitutional and structural questions should not share a crowded ballot
  with routine program reviews.

### Early review trigger

An early review may be opened by a defined combination of:

- verified citizen support across a geographic minimum;
- material cost or outcome deviation;
- an unresolved audit or legal finding;
- legislative referral; or
- a supermajority of the citizen oversight panel.

Missing data alone should normally trigger an evidence order or corrective
period, not immediate termination.

### Emergency action

Emergencies require action before a mass ballot can be informed. Temporary
authority must therefore include scope, expiration, public reasons, spending
records, rights constraints, legislative oversight, and retrospective audit.
Emergency ratification must not become a precondition for urgent life-safety
operations.

## How options reach the ballot

1. **Evidence record:** An independent office publishes the problem, status
   quo, costs, outcomes, uncertainty, rights, legal constraints, and feasible
   range.
2. **Open proposal period:** Citizens, affected communities, agencies,
   legislators, and qualified organizations may submit solutions with funding
   and conflict disclosures.
3. **Qualification:** Objective rules test support, legality, single subject,
   fiscal completeness, implementability, and nonduplication.
4. **Citizen synthesis panel:** A stratified panel hears competing evidence and
   may combine substantially similar proposals into coherent alternatives.
5. **Independent review:** Legal, fiscal, distributional, operational, and
   rights analyses accompany every surviving option.
6. **Published exclusion log:** Every excluded or merged proposal receives a
   reason, source record, and appeal path.
7. **Shortlist ballot:** Voters rank no more than five options, including the
   status quo and “return for revision.” Every option is fully financed on the
   same scoring basis.
8. **Consent round:** The leading change faces the status quo in a final
   majority comparison, either on the same ranked ballot through an explicit
   rule or in a separate runoff.

## Nonremovable choices

The option-selection body may not remove:

- the lawful status quo;
- return for revision;
- a citizen proposal that independently satisfies every published
  qualification rule; or
- a rights-preserving continuity option when termination could interrupt an
  essential service or lawful commitment.

## Why two stages

A ranked shortlist reduces spoilers and lets voters compare coherent packages.
The final change-versus-status-quo test answers a different question: whether
the selected reform has an affirmative mandate to replace current law. Winning
a divided first round is not enough.

## Numeric ballots

Numeric choice is appropriate only when:

- the unit and denominator are unambiguous;
- minimum and maximum bounds are lawful and explained;
- consequences across the range are shown;
- all linked numbers reconcile, such as allocations summing to 100;
- rights and commitments are enforced outside the numeric range; and
- the aggregation rule is fixed before ballots are cast.

For a single rate with roughly single-peaked preferences, the median is a strong
starting candidate because an extreme entry cannot pull it as an average can.
For a 100-point multidimensional budget, there is no equally simple answer:
coordinate medians may not sum to 100, while means are sensitive to intensity
and outliers. RATIFY must compare at least the arithmetic mean, trimmed mean,
geometric median on the 100-point simplex, and state-weighted variants before
choosing a rule.

Field-by-field aggregation must not break fiscal closure. The experiment must
compare complete-ballot methods such as a feasible geometric median, a medoid
drawn from actual balanced ballots, or ranked clusters of coherent packages.

## Ballot-load limits

- No more than five substantive alternatives for one decision.
- No more than a tested reading-time and complexity budget per election.
- Separate ballots or cycles when interactions cannot be explained accurately.
- Offer accessible, multilingual, machine-readable, and printable materials.
- Measure comprehension before deployment; publication alone is not proof of
  understanding.

## Fairness checks

- Who controls agenda access and option wording?
- Can money purchase qualification, visibility, or apparent grassroots support?
- Did merging proposals change their material effect?
- Does ranking method create a different winner from pairwise public preference?
- Can strategic abstention defeat a turnout quorum?
- Does a territorial rule reverse a national majority?
- Are protected groups forced to campaign for continued rights or essential care?
- Can the result actually be implemented and audited on schedule?
