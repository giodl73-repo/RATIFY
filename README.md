# RATIFY

**Research and simulation for citizen lawmaking, program reauthorization, and
durable national consensus.**

**Series:** Election Systems.

RATIFY asks what American government might look like if voters served as an
additional legislative chamber—not merely electing representatives, but
initiating proposals, ratifying major laws, and periodically reviewing public
programs.

## Research program

| Area | Question |
|---|---|
| Comparative systems | Where do initiatives, referendums, repeal votes, and citizen assemblies work, and where do they fail? |
| U.S. mechanisms | Which changes could be statutory, which require state action, and which require constitutional amendment? |
| Consensus rules | Can national equality and geographically broad consent coexist without reproducing Electoral College distortions? |
| Reauthorization | How can voters renew, reform, consolidate, place on probation, phase out, or sunset programs? |
| Experiments | How do popular, state, electoral-weighted, double-majority, and supermajority rules behave under varied electorates? |

## Candidate national rule

The initial design hypothesis is a **double majority**:

1. a majority of participating voters nationally; and
2. approval in a majority of states.

Higher-impact proposals can be tested at stronger thresholds, such as 55% of
the national vote and approval in 30 states. This preserves equal individual
votes in the national count while requiring geographically broad consent.

## Program reauthorization ballot

A program review should offer more than “keep” or “abolish”:

1. reauthorize unchanged;
2. reauthorize with voter-set funding parameters;
3. reauthorize with specified reforms;
4. enter a corrective-performance period;
5. consolidate with another program;
6. sunset gradually; or
7. terminate under a heightened threshold.
